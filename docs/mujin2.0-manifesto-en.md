# 無尽. A Covenant for the Scattered.

---

350,000 international students live and study in Japan today. They came from across Asia and the world, carrying vision and faith. Most have no credit history. No co-signer. No financial record any Japanese bank will recognize. In October 2025, the capital requirement for a Business Manager Visa jumped from ¥5 million to ¥30 million. Students who had spent years building something real found the door closed. Not because they were unworthy. Because the system was never built for them.

---

At the ISM summit, pastors from across Japan described the same thing. Students arrive with gifts, with drive, with faith. They find community in the church, and for many, it is the only place that takes seriously what they are actually facing. But when the real barriers arrive, bank rejections, visa deadlines, incorporation requirements, the church has nothing structural to offer. Visa support was named the most urgent practical need facing international students in Japan. Not a spiritual question. A structural one. The church has the relationship. But relationship alone cannot open a bank account or secure a visa.

---

Mujin is the mechanism. It is a trust-based grant program rooted in Japan's own tradition of 無尽講, the rotating mutual aid circles where communities pooled resources and lifted each other in seasons of need. Not charity. Not debt. Trust made visible through community. Mujin provides ¥500,000 in venture funding to international students with entrepreneurial vision, not as a loan, but as a covenant. Recipients build under structured mentorship. They are measured not on financial history, which they cannot build, but on four behavioral signals: how consistently they show up, how transparently they report, how faithfully they engage their peers, and how honestly they reflect. That record becomes the trust dossier no bank currently holds. When their venture succeeds, they return the principal and a covenant gift to fund the generation that follows. Capital recycles. Trust compounds.

---

The church is not peripheral to this model. It is the foundation. The church already knows who is faithful. It already holds the relationships no algorithm can manufacture. Tokyo's ward governments are actively seeking registered community partners to reach where government cannot. The church is that partner. And the church has its own reason to answer. By 2030, half of Japan's Protestant congregations are projected to close. Aging memberships, declining attendance, communities asking whether their best years are behind them. The international students arriving at Japan's doorstep are not a distraction from that crisis. For many congregations, they are the answer to it. Mujin is not asking the church to serve the scattered. It is inviting the church to be renewed by them.

---

There are two concrete ways to participate. First: identify students in your congregation who carry entrepreneurial vision. Introduce them. Stand as a character witness. Your endorsement is not a formality. It is the trust signal that opens the door. Second: your building is quiet Monday through Friday. For one student, that space, made available as a registered business address at a nominal fee, is the difference between staying in Japan to build and being forced to leave. We are not asking you to become a bank or a landlord. We are asking you to let what you already have do what it was always meant to do.

---

We cannot do this without you. Not because we lack resources. Because trust cannot be manufactured. It must be witnessed. The church is the witness. Join us. Be part of what Japan has been waiting for.
