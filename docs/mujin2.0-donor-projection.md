# 無尽 — Donor Projection
### A ¥158,700,000 ($1,000,000 USD) Capital Case

---

## The Problem

350,000 international students live and study in Japan. Most cannot open a bank account, secure a business address, or qualify for the visa that would let them stay and build. The system was never built for them.

---

## The Model

Mujin provides ¥500,000 in venture funding to international students with entrepreneurial vision. Not as a loan. As a covenant. Upon success, recipients return the principal and a covenant gift — funding the next generation. Capital recycles. Trust compounds.

Operations are fully self-funded. Students pay ¥10,000/month for co-working space. That revenue covers all program costs. Every yen of donor capital goes directly to students.

## Why This Works

**Zero overhead dependency.** Co-working revenue from students covers all operating costs. Donor capital is not diluted by salaries, rent, or administration.

**Self-funded team.** Leadership operates under the Frontier Commons support-raising model. No organizational salary burden.

**Built-in accountability.** The Trust Score system — measuring responsiveness, transparency, mutualism, and reflection — ensures capital goes to students most likely to succeed and return.

**Covenant, not charity.** Recipients are not recipients. They become the next generation's donors. The program does not ask students to pay it back. It invites them to pay it forward.

## Appendix: Assumptions & Data Sources

### Graduation Rate — 45%

Global startup failure data: 20% fail year 1, 45% by year 5, 65% by year 10. Long-term survival rate for ventures is ~10% by conventional definition.

Mujin's graduation gate is not conventional startup success. It requires company incorporation, product live, 3 months non-negative cash flow, and a green trust score for 6 consecutive months. This is a micro-entrepreneur threshold. Small business viability within 3 years runs 40–60% globally. We use 45% as the conservative midpoint, accounting for Japan's higher failure stigma and the complexity of operating as a foreign national.

Upside argument: Mujin's cohort is self-selected and community-filtered. Selection effect should push this above the baseline.

---

### Capital Return Rate — 75% of Graduates

No direct external data source exists for honor pledge return rates. This assumption is derived from three structural factors built into the program: self-selected and community-accountable participants, a social accountability mechanism (exclusion from alumni and university career networks), and ISM community culture emphasizing covenant and mutual obligation.

Range modeled: 60% conservative / 75% moderate / 85% optimistic. Moderate used for base projection.

This is the least data-grounded assumption in the model and should be stress-tested once cohort 1 data exists.

---

### Return Timeline — 24–36 Months

Median time to first revenue: 6–12 months (Kauffman Foundation). Median time to non-negative cash flow: 18–24 months. Mujin's graduation gate requires 3 months non-negative cash flow plus 6 months green trust score. Minimum realistic timeline is 18 months. Buffer added for exit interview and capital return process yields 24–36 months from enrollment.

---

### Co-Working Revenue — ¥10,000/Student/Month

Sourced directly from Mujin program design. Church Usage Agreement at ¥10,000/month satisfies Immigration Bureau physical office requirement for Business Manager Visa. Fixed program design parameter, not an estimate. Average student tenure in program modeled at 20 months.

---

### Deemed Donation — 20%

Sourced directly from Mujin legal structure. Religious Corporation tax framework allows up to 20% of co-working revenue donated tax-free to the Mutual Aid Fund. Fixed legal design parameter, not an estimate.

---

First cohort is relationship-gated through pastor introductions. 15 students requires 2–3 active pastor relationships — achievable within the launch timeline. Ceiling of 50 reflects original pilot target and requires near-full ISM network mobilization.

---

### Entrepreneurship Intent — 35–41%

Sources: Gallup / Lumina Foundation survey data, Kauffman Foundation research. Actual attempt rate is 7–10% — one in ten who express intent follow through. Mujin targets only students with demonstrated entrepreneurial vision, filtered by pastor recommendation, not the general student population.

---

### $1M Raise Plausibility

Three inputs: Andrew Feng's direct knowledge that IFI has raised $1M quickly through the ISM donor network. Frontier Commons self-funded staff model eliminates the largest cost category typical of comparable programs. Original roadmap target of ¥50M confirmed insufficient once operational self-sufficiency through co-working revenue was modeled — $1M is the figure that fully funds the reserve and removes fundraising pressure for 7+ years.

---

### Zero Operational Cost to Donors

Three independent structural confirmations: students pay ¥10,000/month covering 80% of operations through co-working revenue; staff self-fund under the Frontier Commons support-raising model; admin and mentors work remotely or in public spaces with no office overhead. These are program design features, not projections.
